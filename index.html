SCI REFERENCE

What it is: A technique that keeps a donor organ alive and functioning outside the human body by connecting it to a perfusion circuit.

The circuit continuously pumps oxygenated fluid carrying nutrients through the organ — replicating blood flow and allowing metabolic activity to continue.

Key functions:

O₂ delivery waste removal temperature control pressure regulation

Unlike static cold storage, ex vivo perfusion maintains physiological conditions and allows real-time assessment of organ viability.

What they are: Biodegradable, spherical lipid-shell structures designed to encapsulate and transport therapeutic molecules to specific tissue targets.

Their lipid bilayer structure closely resembles biological cell membranes, enabling efficient cellular uptake without immune rejection.

lipid shell therapeutic payload biodegradable targeted delivery

LNPs are clinically validated — the same delivery platform used in mRNA COVID-19 vaccines.

Enhanced Permeability & Retention (EPR): Inflamed or stressed tissues exhibit increased vascular permeability — effectively "leaking" more nanoparticles into the surrounding region.

Stressed cells upregulate surface receptors and stress-response pathways that LNPs bind to preferentially.

vascular permeability ↑ cellular stress signals EPR effect

This creates passive targeting: LNPs concentrate precisely where intervention is needed most, without active guidance.

Targeting precision: Ensuring LNPs reach stressed tissue without off-target deposition requires careful formulation tuning.

Organ variability: Each donor organ presents unique perfusion characteristics — adaptive algorithms must generalize effectively.

Timing: LNP introduction must occur at the correct perfusion phase to maximize uptake before transplant.

precision targeting biological variability safety margin

AI-controlled perfusion: Machine learning models adaptively optimize perfusion parameters in real-time for each unique organ.

Personalized conditioning: LNP payload tailored to donor/recipient immune profiles for maximum compatibility.

Mechanisms

Scientific Principles

Core biological and engineering concepts underpinning the preservation framework.

Perfusion Dynamics

Hemodynamic principles govern how oxygenated fluid distributes through vascular networks. Flow uniformity — measured via pressure gradients and oxygen extraction — determines organ health during ex vivo maintenance.

hemodynamics

Inflammatory Signaling

Ischemia-reperfusion injury triggers cytokine cascades — IL-1β, TNF-α, NFκB pathways — that amplify tissue damage and prime aggressive immune responses post-transplant. Dampening these signals pre-implant reduces downstream rejection risk.

cytokines

LNP Transport Biology

Lipid nanoparticles traverse the vasculature and preferentially accumulate in regions of elevated permeability via the Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) effect — a passive targeting mechanism requiring no active guidance.

EPR effect

Organ Preservation Biology

Cellular viability depends on continuous ATP generation, ion gradient maintenance, and waste clearance. Ex vivo perfusion sustains these processes beyond what static cold storage can achieve — extending the viable preservation window.

cellular viability

Immune Response Modulation

Early post-transplant rejection is driven by damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) released from stressed tissue. Reducing DAMP output during preservation lowers the immunological alarm signal that triggers acute rejection episodes.

DAMPs

Adaptive Regulation

Real-time adjustment of perfusion parameters — flow rate, oxygenation, pressure — based on continuous organ feedback creates a closed-loop system that actively optimizes the preservation environment for each unique organ.

closed-loop control

Horizon

Challenges & Future Applications

Current scientific constraints and the frontier of next-generation transplant engineering.

Research Challenges

  • Targeting precision requires careful LNP formulation to minimize off-target tissue deposition.
  • Organ biological variability means adaptive algorithms must generalize across diverse vascular anatomies.
  • Timing of LNP introduction must be precisely coordinated with the perfusion phase for optimal uptake.
  • Translating ex vivo findings to in vivo outcomes requires controlled clinical validation trials.
  • Manufacturing scalability and sterility constraints for LNP formulations at clinical volume.
  • Ethical considerations around consent frameworks for novel organ conditioning protocols.

Future Applications

  • AI-controlled perfusion systems that autonomously optimize parameters using machine learning on real-time biomarker data.
  • Personalized organ conditioning using donor/recipient immune profile data to tailor LNP payload formulations.
  • Extended donor organ viability windows enabling longer-distance matching and broader organ utilization.
  • Smart nanoparticle systems with programmable release kinetics and multi-target payload delivery.
  • Precision medicine integration connecting transplant conditioning to individualized immunosuppression regimens.
  • Organ repair platforms using ex vivo perfusion to partially reverse ischemic injury before implantation.
